# Cash Advance App UX Research: Tilt, Competitors, and the Psychology of Borrowing

**Bottom Line Up Front:** Tilt (formerly Empower) occupies a volatile position in the cash advance market — beloved by first-time users for its speed and accessibility, but increasingly distrusted by long-term users who encounter opaque limit reductions, subscription cancellation friction, and auto-debit overdraft cascades. Across the entire cash advance category, the core user experience follows a predictable emotional arc: **panic → relief → routine → entrapment → resignation or escape**. The gap between advertised advance maximums and actual first-time offers ($400 marketed vs. $100 average) is the single most corrosive trust issue, and it is industry-wide. This report provides the raw evidence, verbatim user language, and behavioral patterns needed to construct a synthetic user agent that authentically represents how real cash advance users think, feel, and decide.

## SECTION 3: MARKET CONTEXT FINDINGS

### Market size and growth

The U.S. cash advance app market was valued at approximately **$2.1 billion in 2024** and is projected to reach **$5.4 billion by 2033** at a 10.5% CAGR. The CFPB documented explosive growth in employer-partnered EWA: from **$3.2B across 18.6M transactions in 2018** to **$22.8B across 214M transactions in 2022**. The D2C segment (where Tilt competes) saw Dave and MoneyLion alone process **$4.5B in 2022**. The CFPB's December 2025 advisory opinion notes the market is "set to expand by about 300% between 2024 and 2034."

### Who uses these apps

Cash advance users are predominantly **millennials and Gen Z** (ages 18–44), with **80%+ being hourly or gig workers**. **79% of Cash App users have FICO scores below 600**. A DebtHammer survey found **78% of cash advance app users are previous payday loan borrowers**, and **33%+ of all Americans** have signed up for at least one cash advance app. The average user takes **27 EWA transactions per year**, with an average transaction size of **$106** and average annual funds accessed of **$3,000**. Nearly **50% of active users** accessed advances at least once per month in 2022.

Key employment sectors: retail, hospitality, fast food, healthcare, gig/rideshare. Major employers offering EWA include Walmart, Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Kroger, and Wendy's.

### The paycheck-to-paycheck backdrop

**65–67% of Americans live paycheck to paycheck**. **59% lack savings to cover a $1,000 emergency**. **77% of workers would face financial difficulty if their paycheck were delayed one week**. **37% of U.S. adults can't cover a $400 emergency** (Federal Reserve). These statistics define the target user's baseline financial state.

### Regulatory landscape

The regulatory environment is highly contested and shifting rapidly. The **CFPB** has flip-flopped: a 2020 opinion said EWA is not credit; a 2024 Biden-era proposal said it IS credit with illustrative **109.5% APR**; the December 2025 opinion under Acting Director Vought declared "Covered EWA" is NOT credit and tips/fees are not finance charges. **12+ states** have enacted EWA-specific laws, with Nevada's SB 290 becoming the model. However, **6 federal court decisions in 2025** found EWA products to be loans, including *Vickery v. Empower Finance Inc.* in the Northern District of California. Connecticut effectively forced providers out with a 36% APR cap before passing a new EWA framework in June 2025.

### Consumer advocate findings

The **Center for Responsible Lending** (October 2024) analyzed 214,000+ advances across 20 apps and found: high-frequency users represent **38% of users but 86% of all advances**; nearly **half of all borrowers used multiple companies in the same month**; app use was **associated with increased overdraft fees and payday loan use**. They termed EWA "a loan shark in your pocket" with "triple-digit annual interest rates." The **NCLC** called the December 2025 CFPB opinion "flatly wrong." A DebtHammer survey found **31% struggled to repay** as scheduled, **17% skipped another bill** to repay, and **4% borrowed from a different app** to repay the first.

### Tilt/Empower company profile

Founded 2016 in San Francisco by Warren Hogarth (ex-Sequoia Capital). **3+ million active subscribers**, **5+ million total customers**, profitable since 2022. Post-money valuation approximately **$797M** (August 2025). Total equity funding ~$174.5M (Sequoia Capital, Initialized Capital, Icon Ventures, Defy Partners). Rebranded from Empower to Tilt in August 2025. Products include cash advance ($10–$400), Line of Credit/Thrive ($200–$1,000), Tilt Credit Cards (launched Aug 2025, issued by WebBank), budgeting tools, AutoSave, and credit monitoring. Subscription: $8/month. Average first-time advance: **$100**. Average returning customer advance: **$174**. Approval rate: **75%**. Operations also in Mexico.

## SECTION 4: EMOTIONAL & BEHAVIORAL PATTERNS

### The emotional arc: Panic → Relief → Routine → Entrapment → Resignation or Escape

**Stage 1 — The triggering event (desperation/urgency):** Users almost always start in response to an acute financial shock. The emotional tone is panic, urgency, and a sense of having no other option.

- **Runeda (EarnIn user, single mom):** "So, uh, my son wakes up, uh, really early sometimes, and it was like five o'clock in the morning. And I went to try to open an app on my phone and it wasn't loading. And I was like, did they turn my internet off?... They told me I had to pay like over $200. I had like 50 bucks in my bank account. That was the first domino and everything else just kind of fell apart from there."
- EBRI/Fourth (Fall 2024): **76% of hospitality workers** cited food as the most common reason for accessing earned wages; **47% cited rent/housing**.

**Stage 2 — First use (relief and endorphin hit):** The first experience is overwhelmingly positive — fast, easy, private.

- **Lee (DailyPay user, teacher):** "I have money in my account before payday, right? It's almost like endorphins."
- **Lee on first impression:** "I didn't see it as a loan. You're not like signing stuff, you know, you're not sitting and reading through a loan agreement. It's just like, it's instant. It's fast and it seems cheap."
- Financial expert: "Payday loans threaten you with penalties. Cash advance apps charm you into a habit."

**Stage 3 — The repeat-use descent (tool to crutch to trap):** What starts as a one-time emergency tool becomes habitual within weeks.

- Average EWA user takes **36 advances per year** (vs. 8 for a payday loan consumer)
- **75% of hospitality workers** accessed earned wages at least weekly; 58% several times per week
- **75% took out at least one advance on the same day or day after repayment**
- **53% borrowed from multiple apps** during their first year
- **Runeda's cycle:** "[After the internet bill], that setback meant Runeda had to borrow again just 3 days later for gas, and 4 days after that for a utility bill. Which meant her next paycheck was smaller, which meant she ended up borrowing again 3 more times over the next week."
- **Runeda:** "You don't have room to breathe, it's like you can't breathe. Every dollar is accounted for. It leaves no room for error."
- **Smith (7–8 apps simultaneously):** "It just went completely out of control, and I was having to live off the borrowing apps."

### Shame and stigma

Users carry deep shame about needing cash advances. The privacy of apps is both a draw and a mask.

- **Lee (teacher):** "When you go to the payday loan, you are kind of watching your backs. Who's seeing me walk into this building? But the cash advance, uh, apps, it's private. There's this huge social stigma against people who are poor. Everyone I know is in debt. But we have to pretend like we're not. And there's a lot of shame wrapped up in that."
- **57% of hospitality EWA users** said it helped them avoid borrowing from friends and family — a signal that social stigma around borrowing is a primary motivator
- **Tilt user:** "No one likes having to borrow money from friends or family! Now I can just borrow from myself through Tilt!"
- Users actively resist framing advances as "loans": "You're not loaning me, you know, the money, it is mine. I earned it."

### Trust formation and destruction

**What builds trust:** Visual design (clean, modern); app store presence; "no interest" framing; no credit check; privacy; employer sponsorship.

**What destroys trust:** Manipulative tipping (EarnIn's 13-click avoidance path; Dave's sad animated child); unexpected subscription charges; lower-than-advertised advance amounts; inability to reach human support; overdraft cascades from auto-debit.

- **Runeda on tipping:** "The audacity. I am here to borrow money. I don't have any to give you."
- **Lee on feeling deceived:** "I feel deceived by it, it's crazy. It's absolutely insane."
- **EarnIn reviewer:** "I turned on the overdraft protection shield and without my knowledge it tipped the app automatically which is supposed to be optional... that felt like a violation of my trust."

## SECTION 5: SYNTHESIZED RESEARCH OUTPUT

### RESEARCH SUMMARY

The cash advance app market serves a population defined not by financial irresponsibility but by **structural income insufficiency** — 65%+ of Americans living paycheck to paycheck, the majority unable to absorb a $400 emergency. Tilt occupies a middle position: faster and more accessible than banks, cheaper and less predatory than payday loans, but increasingly suffering from the same trust erosion affecting every competitor. The fundamental product tension is between the user's acute need for money NOW and the app's risk management algorithms that produce opaque, seemingly arbitrary eligibility decisions. Users who are approved and funded quickly become vocal advocates; users who encounter limit drops, subscription confusion, or auto-debit overdrafts become vocally hostile — and they disproportionately seek out complaint platforms. The most dangerous UX pattern is the **expectation-reality gap**: $400 advertised maximum vs. $100 first-time average, compounded by unexplained limit reductions that punish loyal users. For synthetic agent construction, the critical insight is that these users are not casual consumers — they are people in acute financial stress making high-stakes decisions on small screens, often at 5 AM, often for their children, and the emotional register is not curiosity but survival.

### KEY USER ARCHETYPES

**Archetype 1: "The Scrambler"**
- **Core job-to-be-done:** Cover an immediate, specific expense (rent, car repair, utility bill) before payday
- **Mental model of cash advance:** "Emergency bridge money — not a loan, just accessing what I've already earned"
- **Typical emotional state when opening the app:** Panic mixed with hope; "I need this NOW or something terrible happens"
- **Most likely friction point:** Advance amount too low to cover the actual expense ($100 offered for a $300 car repair)
- **Representative quote:** "I had like 50 bucks in my bank account. That was the first domino and everything else just kind of fell apart from there." [VERBATIM — Runeda, EarnIn user]

**Archetype 2: "The Repeat Rider"**
- **Core job-to-be-done:** Smooth out chronic cash flow gaps every pay cycle
- **Mental model of cash advance:** "A regular part of my financial infrastructure — like having a credit card, but for people like me who can't get one"
- **Typical emotional state when opening the app:** Resigned routine mixed with low-grade anxiety about whether the amount will be enough
- **Most likely friction point:** Unexplained advance limit decrease after months of perfect repayment; subscription charging when service isn't accessible
- **Representative quote:** "Been using Empower/Tilt for about 3 years now. I definitely depend on my advances every month and they always seem to come at a time when I need them the most. My only complaint is the sudden drop in my cash advance amount like over a year ago." [VERBATIM — paigecash, App Store]

**Archetype 3: "The App-Hopper"**
- **Core job-to-be-done:** Maximize total advance capacity by stacking multiple apps simultaneously
- **Mental model of cash advance:** "A portfolio of liquidity sources — if one app cuts me off, I have backups"
- **Typical emotional state when opening the app:** Calculated, slightly numb; this is a system they've optimized
- **Most likely friction point:** One app's auto-debit causing insufficient funds for another app's repayment, triggering overdraft cascades
- **Representative quote:** "It just went completely out of control, and I was having to live off the borrowing apps." [VERBATIM — Smith, Nasdaq/NerdWallet interview]

**Archetype 4: "The Cautious First-Timer"**
- **Core job-to-be-done:** Explore whether this type of app is legitimate and safe before committing
- **Mental model of cash advance:** "Skeptical but curious — is this really no-interest? What's the catch?"
- **Typical emotional state when opening the app:** Wary hope mixed with shame about needing this at all
- **Most likely friction point:** Discovering the $8 subscription fee relative to a $10 first advance; feeling trapped by auto-enrollment
- **Representative quote:** "I am rating this app 2 stars for now due to I did have to pay 8 bucks for the first month just to get an advance of 10 dollars, I'm a bit confused on that." [VERBATIM — April Workman, App Store]

### INSIGHT CARDS

**Card 1: The $8-for-$10 Shock**
- **Insight:** First-time users experience severe value mismatch when paying an $8 subscription to access a $10 advance, creating an immediate trust deficit
- **Evidence:** "I did have to pay 8 bucks for the first month just to get an advance of 10 dollars" — App Store, 2-star review
- **Journey stage:** Onboarding / First advance
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Consider waiving or reducing the first subscription payment, or ensuring minimum first advance is at least 3x the subscription fee; communicate the expected advance amount BEFORE charging subscription

**Card 2: The Opaque Algorithm Wall**
- **Insight:** Users experience advance limit decreases as arbitrary punishment despite perfect repayment history; customer service cannot explain why, destroying trust in the system's fairness
- **Evidence:** "$175 → $75 for no reason, and it stayed that way consistently for over a year... customer service could not give any insight either" — paigecash, App Store; "dropped my advance limit from $200 to $75! I now have 3 overdraft fees of $35, and a negative bank balance of over $395!!" — Victor A, Trustpilot
- **Journey stage:** Repeat use
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Provide transparent limit-change explanations ("Your limit changed because...") even if generic; give users clear pathways to restore previous limits; never decrease limits without notification

**Card 3: The Overdraft Domino Effect**
- **Insight:** Auto-debit repayment timing misaligns with user's actual payday, causing cascading overdraft fees that can exceed the original advance amount by 3–5x
- **Evidence:** "Empower autodebited my bank account. They got paid, but I got an overdraft fee... The overdraft had a domino effect on my bank account, resulting in additional overdrafts" — Victor A, Trustpilot; "the app sets repayments before payday, expecting a $15 bank hit unless the date is shifted" — CashAdvanceApps.com
- **Journey stage:** Repayment
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Implement real-time balance checking before auto-debit; provide prominent, early reminders about rescheduling repayment dates; default to post-deposit timing rather than pre-deposit

**Card 4: The Cancellation Trap**
- **Insight:** Users who want to stop using the service encounter persistent subscription charges, buried cancel options, and requirements to pay outstanding balances before closing — creating a sense of being trapped
- **Evidence:** "I've deleted and unsubscribed from the app but still being charged" — BBB; "when I try to cancel my account I'm told I owe money" — Kyra Rose, Trustpilot; "they continued to take their sub fee of $8 every month for 6 months despite the account no longer being eligible" — Google Play
- **Journey stage:** Support / Churn
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Make cancellation one-tap and immediate; stop subscription charges the moment a user initiates cancellation; provide clear confirmation of cancellation with expected timeline

**Card 5: The Support Black Hole**
- **Insight:** Customer service is predominantly chatbot-driven with scripted responses and no human escalation path, which is catastrophic for users experiencing financial emergencies caused by the app itself
- **Evidence:** "Customer service zero help. They just spout out prefabricated script" — ComplaintsBoard; "The chatbot kept repeating the same canned response with no option to reach a real person" — Nicholas Baldwin, Trustpilot; "I communicated with some 8-10 people at Empower, the last one asked for a screenshot of my account, but never got back to me" — Victor A
- **Journey stage:** Support
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Create an escalation path for financial-harm issues (overdrafts, missing funds, billing errors) that bypasses the chatbot; flag accounts with active overdraft complaints for priority human review

**Card 6: The Rebrand Rupture**
- **Insight:** The Empower→Tilt rebrand (August 2025) caused widespread technical failures — payment processing errors, locked accounts, verification loops, lost transaction history — and felt to users like abandonment
- **Evidence:** "All of a sudden they switched to Tilt and updated the app and every single one of my payments have failed... I have attempted to make OVER 50 payments on the app in the past 3 months" — BBB; "Empower was a wonderful service however when they decided to rebrand thats when all of the trouble started" — Renee Lindsey
- **Journey stage:** Repeat use / Support
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** For any future platform migration, implement parallel-run periods; pre-migrate all payment methods and verification states; provide dedicated migration support channel

**Card 7: The Advertised Maximum Mirage**
- **Insight:** Marketing prominently features "up to $400" but the average first-time advance is $100. This gap is the single most common source of user disappointment across the entire category.
- **Evidence:** Tilt's own disclosure: average first-time advance $100–$102; "I'm on my 4th advance with Empower and they still have not offered me more than $25" — Google Play via Millennial Money; FTC found Dave users experienced identical gap ($500 advertised, $25–$73 typical)
- **Journey stage:** Awareness / Onboarding
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Lead marketing with average advance amounts, not maximums; show personalized estimated advance range during onboarding BEFORE collecting subscription payment

**Card 8: The Privacy-as-Dignity Factor**
- **Insight:** For many users, the ability to borrow privately — without facing a loan officer, visiting a physical location, or asking family — is the primary value proposition, not the advance amount itself
- **Evidence:** "No one likes having to borrow money from friends or family! Now I can just borrow from myself through Tilt!" — Trustpilot; "When you go to the payday loan, you are kind of watching your backs. Who's seeing me walk into this building?" — Lee, teacher; "I felt appreciated and could just be myself and no worries of being abused or embarrassed or belittled" — Trustpilot
- **Journey stage:** Awareness / Onboarding
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Reinforce dignity and privacy throughout the UX; avoid language that feels like debt collection; ensure app notifications are discreet and don't reveal financial status

**Card 9: The Installment Advantage**
- **Insight:** Tilt's 4-installment repayment option is a genuine differentiator that users cite as superior to payday loans and some competitors, because it prevents the "payday evaporation" effect
- **Evidence:** "the best part is you don't have to repay it all on the next paycheck like a payday loan.. they break up the payments up to 4 installments which is extremely helpful" — Hatchetface81, App Store
- **Journey stage:** Repayment
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Prominently feature installment repayment in marketing and onboarding; default to installments rather than lump sum; show projected paycheck impact of each repayment option

**Card 10: The Catch-22 Eligibility**
- **Insight:** Users who most need a cash advance (those with low or negative balances) are the least likely to qualify, creating a fundamental product paradox that generates intense frustration
- **Evidence:** "The criteria for a cash advance is written specifically for people that wouldn't even need a cash advance in the first place. You have to maintain a $50 balance for so many days" — ComplaintsBoard; "The irony is that if I HAD money in my bank account, the necessity to use Float Me wouldn't exist" — FloatMe user, App Store
- **Journey stage:** Eligibility / Onboarding
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Communicate eligibility requirements clearly BEFORE subscription enrollment; consider balance-trend analysis rather than point-in-time balance checks; provide alternative pathways for users who narrowly miss criteria

**Card 11: The No-Tip Differentiator**
- **Insight:** Users who've experienced tip pressure on competitor apps (Dave, EarnIn) specifically praise Tilt for NOT pressuring tips — this is a meaningful trust signal
- **Evidence:** "I also like the fact that they don't pressure you for 'tips' like other cash advance apps do" — paigecash, App Store; "The audacity. I am here to borrow money. I don't have any to give you" — Runeda, on tip pressure; EarnIn requires 13 clicks to avoid tipping
- **Journey stage:** First advance / Repeat use
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Continue and actively market the no-tip policy; position it as a trust differentiator vs. competitors in onboarding

**Card 12: The State Availability Blindspot**
- **Insight:** Users subscribe and pay the $8 monthly fee before discovering the cash advance feature isn't available in their state
- **Evidence:** "Empower allowed me to subscribe knowing that Cash Advance—the main feature promoted—was not available in [my state]. That was the only reason I signed up" — BBB complaint
- **Journey stage:** Onboarding
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Check state eligibility for cash advance BEFORE allowing subscription enrollment; display state-specific feature availability prominently in the signup flow

**Card 13: The Speed Endorphin**
- **Insight:** Instant delivery (within minutes) creates a powerful positive emotional response that drives repeat use and word-of-mouth; users who experience fast funding become the strongest advocates
- **Evidence:** "I linked my new debit card and within a matter of seconds they transferred the money. I mean it literally was that fast" — Google Play; "It's like asking mom for money!" — Trustpilot; "I didn't see it as a loan... it's instant. It's fast and it seems cheap" — Lee, DailyPay user
- **Journey stage:** First advance / Repeat use
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Optimize instant delivery as the default experience; consider absorbing some instant delivery fees for first-time users to create a strong positive first impression

**Card 14: The Plaid Connection Fragility**
- **Insight:** Bank connection via Plaid is a recurring failure point that blocks advance access for weeks, with customer service unable to resolve it
- **Evidence:** "Customer service tells me it's issues with plaid connecting to my account and it will be fixed within 48 hours. That was 6 weeks ago. Funny no other app using plaid and my bank account has any issue" — ComplaintsBoard
- **Journey stage:** Onboarding / Repeat use
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Build redundant bank connection methods beyond Plaid; provide manual verification fallbacks; set honest ETAs for Plaid reconnection issues

**Card 15: The Dependency Awareness Gap**
- **Insight:** Users recognize intellectually that cash advance apps can become a cycle but feel unable to stop because each paycheck arrives already depleted by repayment
- **Evidence:** "It's also dangerous to get into this cycle of living beyond your means. I can draw $1000 from my next paycheck and it's dangerous to have your next paycheck instantly depleted and then you have to keep using it to not run out of money" — EarnIn reviewer; "I got tired of my paychecks being short after taking the advance, but then I sometimes couldn't make it to the next payday either" — Tomika Wright; 75% took out a new advance on the same day or day after repayment (CRL)
- **Journey stage:** Repeat use
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Build opt-in financial health nudges showing cumulative usage patterns; offer "cool down" periods; show projected paycheck after repayment before confirming each advance; consider graduated limits for high-frequency users

**Card 16: The Subscription-While-Denied Outrage**
- **Insight:** Charging the $8 monthly subscription while simultaneously denying a user's advance request is perceived as the single most dishonest thing the app does
- **Evidence:** "Tilt IMMEDIATELY takes money out of your account if you link a bank account when applying for a cash advance. They immediately deny you for the advance... and they still fraudulently take funds" — BBB; "They cold-heartedly trap people into paying $11 or so a month in hopes they might qualify for advances in the future" — ComplaintsBoard
- **Journey stage:** Onboarding / Eligibility
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Design implication:** Do not charge subscription until the user has been confirmed eligible for at least one advance; refund the first month's subscription if no advance is approved within 30 days

**Card 17: The Pay Frequency Misdetection**
- **Insight:** The app's automated pay frequency detection incorrectly identifies some users' pay schedules, leading to wrong repayment dates and blocked advances — and support cannot fix it
- **Evidence:** "The app incorrectly shows I'm paid monthly when I'm actually paid bimonthly, and customer support could not fix it" — Nicholas Baldwin, Trustpilot
- **Journey stage:** Onboarding / Repayment
- **Severity:** Significant
- **Design implication:** Allow manual pay schedule override; confirm detected pay frequency with the user before setting repayment dates; build an easy in-app correction flow

### OPEN QUESTIONS

1. **What exactly triggers limit decreases?** No user or support agent can explain the algorithm. Is it based on bank balance trends, spending patterns, external data, or random variation? This opacity is the #1 driver of repeat-user frustration.

2. **What is the actual retention curve?** High app store ratings suggest strong early experience, but the 3.1 recent score and BBB complaints suggest high dissatisfaction among longer-term users. What percentage of subscribers are still active after 6 months? After 12?

3. **How does Tilt's rebrand affect new user acquisition vs. legacy trust?** The empower.me Trustpilot page (1.5 stars) still ranks in search results. Is this cannibalizing new user trust?

4. **What is the subscription cancellation completion rate?** Multiple users report being unable to cancel. What percentage of cancellation attempts succeed on the first try?

5. **How many users experience overdraft fees directly caused by Tilt's auto-debit timing?** This is the most financially harmful UX failure and the one most likely to generate regulatory scrutiny.

6. **What is the rate of "phantom advances" (charged but not received)?** Multiple BBB complaints describe being charged repayment for advances that never deposited. Is this a systemic issue or isolated?

7. **How do users perceive the Thrive Line of Credit vs. the Cash Advance?** Is there confusion between the two products? Do users understand the differences?

8. **What is the user overlap between Tilt and competitor apps?** CRL found 50% use multiple apps simultaneously. What percentage of Tilt users are also using Dave, EarnIn, or Brigit?

9. **How does the "250+ non-traditional financial health signals" model perform for gig workers and irregular-income users specifically?** These are the highest-need users — are they being systematically underserved?

10. **What is the emotional impact of the credit-building features?** Are users who see credit score improvements via Tilt more loyal and less likely to churn?
